A Note on Capital Depreciation in the Overlapping Generations Model

Michael Kotrous Email: michael.kotrous@uga.edu John Munro Godfrey, Sr. Department of Economics
University of Georgia
(April 22, 2026)

In standard presentations of the overlapping generations model pioneered by Samuelson (1958) and Diamond (1965),111See Chapter 2 of Williamson (1999). there is an implicit simplifying assumption that there is no depreciation of capital. The purpose of this simplifying assumption is to avoid complications related to the timing of depreciation, to be discussed below.

The assumption of no depreciation can be inferred from the aggregate feasibility constraint (in the planner’s setting), or the market clearing condition for the consumption good (in the competitive equilibrium setting):

F⁢(Kt,Lt)+Kt=Kt+1+Lt⁢cty+Lt−1⁢cto

How do we modify the model if we wish to not impose the restriction that δ=0? In the social planner’s problem, you simply need to incorporate capital depreciation into the aggregate feasibility constraint. For δ∈(0,1], the planner’s constraint becomes:

F⁢(Kt,Lt)+(1−δ)⁢Kt=Kt+1+Lt⁢cty+Lt−1⁢cto.

In the competitive equilibrium setting, you need to:

  1. 1.

    Adjust the consumption market clearing condition:

    F⁢(Kt,Lt)+(1−δ)⁢Kt=Kt+1+Lt⁢cty+Lt−1⁢cto.
  2. 2.

    Adjust the capital income available to an agent in old age through their budget constraint:

    co=s⁢(1+r−δ),

    where r−δ is the net return.

To understand the second point, start with the firm problem, which is unchanged.

maxKt,Nt⁡F⁢(Kt,Nt)−r⁢Kt−w⁢Nt,

with optimality conditions

r=FK⁢(Kt,Nt)=f′⁢(kt)w=FN⁢(Kt,Nt)=f⁢(kt)−kt×f′⁢(kt)

where f is the intensive form of the CRS production function F. When incorporating positive capital depreciation into the OLG model, we need to be clear that r is the gross return and r−δ is the net return on savings.

The implicit timing assumption in this setup is that depreciation occurs after production takes place and before the factor payment for capital is made to old-age households. Walk through the sequence of events in period t:

  1. 1.

    The economy begins with capital stock

    Kt=Lt−1⁢st−1.
  2. 2.

    All capital is used in production, yielding F⁢(Kt,Lt). Production destroys capital at rate δ.

  3. 3.

    After production, firm makes payments to workers (the young) and capital owners (the old).222We assumed CRS technology, implying zero profits. Total resources available to finance these payments are:

    F⁢(Kt,Lt)+(1−δ)⁢Kt.

    Labor income to the young is:

    Lt⁢w=Lt⁢(f⁢(k)−k⁢f′⁢(k)).

    Capital income to the old is:

    Lt−1⁢st−1−δ⁢Lt−1⁢st−1+r⁢Lt−1⁢st−1 =Lt−1⁢st−1⁢(1+r−δ)
    =Kt⁢(1+f′⁢(k)−δ)

Adding labor and capital payments demonstrates that national income accounting identities hold:

Lt⁢(f⁢(k)−k⁢f′⁢(k))+Kt⁢(1+f′⁢(k)−δ) =F⁢(Kt,Lt)−Kt⁢f′⁢(k)+Kt+Kt⁢f′⁢(k)−δ⁢Kt
=F⁢(Kt,Lt)+(1−δ)⁢Kt

Finally, since aggregate capital payments imply each old agent receives s⁢(1+r−δ), the old-age budget constraint is:

co=s⁢(1+r−δ),

which is the very modification to the household problem stated in point 2 above.

For a more technical discussion of the OLG model, see Mukoyama (2020).
